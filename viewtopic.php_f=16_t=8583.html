Black holes aren't really holes and they don't "go" anywhere.

They are former Suns that swelled so large in mass and then collapsed on themselves causing a tremendous amount of gravity.

Imagine taking all the mass of Earth and cramming it into something size of a pencil eraser. The gravity produced by such a collapse is sufficient to trap light or alter the path of light traveling near the rim or "Event Horizon" of the black hole. Only light travels fast enough to pass the rim and escape out the other side (still having been affected, granted). But only if it's near the very edge. If it shoots too far near the center, it gets sucked in just like everything else.

And because of the immense gravity, objects are accelerated to near-light speeds which means their time slows down (because time is relative and the faster you move, the slower time appears to you as compared with people moving slower than you -- Theory of Relativity 101). So if you managed to get trapped into the event of a black hole, you would continue to accelerate until time nearly stopped for you. If, hypothetically, you managed to make it back out, the rest of the universe would be much, much older than you (how old would depend on how far and long you stayed in the event of the black hole).

That's the theory, at least. Relativity has not yet been sufficiently proven, though there is tons of circumstantial evidence to back it up. But already there are many flaws in it as demonstrated by Quantum Mechanics. There is no unified theory that ties together the elements of Relativity and the non-deterministic nature of quantum (sub-atomic) behavior.

Though Nasa was conducting an expiriment where they sent a precisely calibrated clock on the shuttle and left one (with the same precise time as it's sister clock) back here on Earth.

When the Shuttle got back, if Relativity is true, then the clock on the shuttle would be a few nanoseconds (or perhaps even milliseconds) behind the one left here on Earth.

I'm not sure which Shuttle is was on (Endeavor or Columbia), but i haven't heard the results, so I'm afraid it might have been Columbia
