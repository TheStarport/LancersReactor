@WLB. Apologies, I rambled on a bit on this one.

I have not owned a Dell laptop so I cannot speak from experience. My employer provides me with an IBM laptop and I have a personal Dell desktop at home.

Their laptops usually get good reviews. E.G. The November issue of PC World, for example, rated the Latitude D600 #2 of the top 7 (Power Notebook Class) behind IBM but ahead of HP's NX7000. (But the HP had the biggest screen of the bunch at 15.4", Dell's only had a 14" and real estate is important in laptops).

The Inspiron 5100 was rated #8 of the top 8 behind the second of two IBMs in its class (Value Notebook). HP did not place.

My personal experience with Dell drawbacks ~ NOTE: I don't think any of the things below are relevant to laptops as, well, there is very little you can do on your own without running the risk of breaking something "under the hood" in very cramped quarters. What can be swapped out by the consumer is already made that way. I.e., flop and optical drives, PC cards, etc. And USB 2.0 adds new dimensions to hot swappable peripherals.

1) You cannot modify a Dell without breaching the warranties.

2) While most industry standard upgradeable parts seem to be compatible with a Dell, Dell will NOT support you after you upgrade except for those upgrade parts you purchase from Dell and which are, therefore, Dell warrantied (even for things like RAM, video cards, modem cards, etc.)

3) The motherboard IS NOT industry standard, the PC case IS NOT industry standard in that the standoffs and such are not located in the same spots so you cannot swap out MBs.

4) NO OVERCLOCKING ALLOWED. Dell BIOS like IBM and other big makes, specifically exclude overclocking features.

@WLB,

Hi, sorry for not responding sooner. The wife is very much under the weather lately and only just now getting better so I've been tied up being Mr. Mom and Mr. Nurse for wifey and baby.

Ummm. Yeah. The thing about laptops is that you can do really really worse than Dell or IBM or HP/Compaq or Toshiba but you just won't do as well as you could with a desktop. That's just a fact of computing life.

As for HD speeds, you won't find a laptop HD that goes anywhere near as fast as a full sized desktop HD can. The 5400rpm is very fast for a laptop. (Note: Greater speed means more juice consumed and more heat produced - both difficult handling issues for a laptop.)

I have, for a while now, toyed with going the Alienware laptop way. What holds me back is that, for the money, I could get an ever more killer Alienware desktop, etc., etc., etc. You'll have to decide whether it is money well spent if you go with the Alienware. It IS really kewl though!

Dell is OK. HP also is OK. Neither are the best as there is no best. There are many out there that are worse and only a few that are comparable. Since I like lots of screen space, I would go with the best value 17" laptop. I also would make it a habit of being gentle with the equipment and practice good cooling behavior in terms of setting up the laptop. Heat is more of an enemy of laptops than people realize. Mobility tends to imply a very broad spectrum of ruggedness but, in fact, laptops... especially cutting edge ones, are not that rugged.

If you want affordably rugged but not cutting edge, try a Panasonic Tough Book. You'll notice that it is not as fast or sexy as the latest from other companies. But that "behind the curve" nature of it is for a reason.
