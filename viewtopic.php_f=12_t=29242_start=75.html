@Arch,

As backgrounder only, not as justification or argument for or against....

There is a historical, social and political tradition here that exists both consciouisly and subconsciously that has to do with ideals of self-reliance, independence, civil liberties, and mistrust of big government (as you likely already know).

Indeed the NRA has pretty much touched on all of these principles but no matter how politicized their commentary may be, there are a significant number of people who consciously hold to these notions as inherent and indistinguishable from a free and democratic society. And there is another significant number who adhere to some of these ideals at a subconscious level or consider them subjectively in respect to a dichotomy between what is good for "me" versus what is good for the rest of the country.

Police are welcome to keep crime at bay but they are not seen as being available at the very moment in time when the crime may be committed. In the frame of mind described, the armed citizen and would be victim is able to take action on his own to stop the crime "dead" so to speak (more or less part of ssjag's point).

Although the argument has been that in the colonial and frontier days, each citizen had to be his own law in order to survive and that today's modern conditions militate against this type of thinking, the fact is that there is a very strong appeal that is tied to believing that, living freely includes being able to take necessary defensive measures into ones own hands.

Most states in the US have laws that do not condone the use of deadly force to stop "any" crime. But it varies as to what conditions would justify the use of deadly force. In New York, one only may use deadly force in self defense when it is clear that you yourself are being confronted by deadly force. I.e., if a guy tries to pick your pocket, you cannot shoot him dead. If a guy tries to stab you with his knife, you can shoot him in the act.

In Texas, if you discover an uninvited person in your home at night, you may use deadly force... end of story. In New York, you cannot unless you can show that you yourself were in mortal danger at the time.
