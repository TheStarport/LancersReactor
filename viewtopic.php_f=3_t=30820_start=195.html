Proper physics is all well and good but..

- Speeds would be fantastic. There would be no way of interacting with the
vast majority of the ships you saw, because they would flash past in an
instant. All of the action would have to happen close to stations.

- Most of the time, every other ship or object in the game would be a dot. It
is around 300,000 miles just from Earth to the Moon - and if we say that
a spoaceship turns into a dot at 300 miles distance, that means that the system maps would be huge, but 99.99% empty.

- Deceleration would be a huge problem. 5 seconds late and you'd be *splat*
straight into a planet. 5 seconds early and you still end up miles away.
With a solar system like ours, you would be looking
at fantastic distances between 'interesting' objects, and hence you would
need fantastic speeds to get anywhere.

Trust me, it would take the fun out of it.. Personally I think it's been done
in freelancer really well. There is the impression of being in space without
the inconvienient reality.

athulin

Thu May 12, 2005 8:36 am by athulin

Freelancer 2 could do with some tweaks to game style.
For instance, the story line is, fundamentally, about
making a difference (or a contribution) to Sirius. But once
the story is over, there not much difference to be made anymore.
There's no end to Liberty Rogues or Xenos, and it doesn't
matter how many diamonds you deliver to Manhattan, the
demand doesn't change.

Making a difference is closely related to a more realistic
economy (demand and supply), but it reappears in several
other places.

Reputation building needs an overhaul. Delivering
commodities (in particular needed commodities like
oxygen to space stations) should help change reputation.
This would also help making trading a more interesting
activity, particularly if the trader had some extra tools
of the trade, for instance an autopilot that doesn't need
to be told to go on to the next waypoint ...

Individual reputation would be interesting: a
huge, timely delivery of oxygen might make you at least
a neutral party in some places ... for a while. If commodity
prices were related to reputation, which doesn't seem altogether
impossible, reputation tweaking could also make sense, also
in a larger perspective: if your faction carries a lot
of, say, light arms from Trenton, getting more friendly with
Universal is economically sound. And that may be another kind of
difference on a more global scale.

Yet another kind of difference is the game infrastructure
itself: I find it somewhat odd to have the same spoken accent
all over Sirius. Recording everything in different dialects would
make the game too expensive: this might be one of those things
that could be left to the fan base -- it's just that the game
infrastructure must be prepared to accomodate 200+ different
forms of gretings instead of just five or six, and that takes another
kind of game design.
